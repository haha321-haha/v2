Prostaglandins: The "Culprit" Behind Menstrual Pain

Under the action of prostaglandins, uterine muscles contract to help shed and expel the endometrium. However, excessive prostaglandins cause excessive or abnormally strong uterine contractions, leading to local ischemia and severe spasmodic pain.

üí° Key Mechanisms

  • Prostaglandin E2 (PGE2): Primary pain mediator, causes vasodilation and pain sensitivity
  • Prostaglandin F2Œ± (PGF2Œ±): Causes strong uterine contractions, leading to ischemic pain
  • Leukotrienes: Aggravate inflammatory response, prolonging pain duration

Complex Effects of Hormonal Fluctuations

Changes in relative levels of estrogen and progesterone may intensify the uterine response to prostaglandins, making dysmenorrhea more severe. During perimenopause, dramatic fluctuations of these hormones may also lead to changes or worsening of dysmenorrhea patterns.

üè• Dysmenorrhea Management in Specific Situations

Dysmenorrhea Management for IUD Users

üìã Characteristics of IUD-Related Dysmenorrhea

  • Copper IUD: May increase menstrual flow and dysmenorrhea severity
  • Hormonal IUD: Usually reduces dysmenorrhea, but may cause irregular bleeding initially
  • Adaptation period: 3-6 months after insertion for adaptation

Management Strategies

  • Medication: NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) most effective
  • Heat therapy: Local heat application to relieve uterine spasms
  • Regular follow-up: Check IUD position every 6 months
  • Watch for abnormalities: Severe pain may indicate IUD displacement

Pregnancy-Related Pain Management

‚ö†Ô∏è Special Considerations for Pregnancy Pain

Dysmenorrhea-like pain during pregnancy requires special vigilance and may indicate:

  • Threatened abortion or miscarriage
  • Ectopic pregnancy
  • Placental abruption
  • Signs of preterm labor

Safe Management Principles

  • Immediate medical attention: Any abdominal pain during pregnancy should be evaluated promptly
  • Avoid self-medication: Most pain medications are contraindicated during pregnancy
  • Safe methods: Warm baths, gentle massage, rest
  • Close monitoring: Record pain characteristics and associated symptoms

Perimenopausal Dysmenorrhea Management

Characteristics of Perimenopausal Dysmenorrhea

  • Hormonal fluctuations: Unstable estrogen levels
  • Symptom changes: Dysmenorrhea patterns may change
  • New diseases: Increased risk of uterine fibroids, adenomyosis
  • Psychological factors: Menopausal syndrome affects pain perception

Comprehensive Management Strategies

  • Hormonal assessment: Check FSH, LH, estrogen levels
  • Imaging studies: Ultrasound to rule out organic lesions
  • Hormone therapy: Consider hormone replacement therapy when necessary
  • Lifestyle: Regular exercise, balanced diet, stress management

‚ö†Ô∏è When Surgical Treatment is Needed

Surgical Indications

  • Severe endometriosis: Conservative treatment ineffective
  • Adenomyosis: Severe symptoms affecting quality of life
  • Uterine fibroids: Large or multiple fibroids
  • Congenital anomalies: Uterine or vaginal developmental abnormalities

Surgical Options

  • Conservative surgery: Laparoscopic lesion excision
  • Uterine artery embolization: Suitable for uterine fibroids
  • Hysterectomy: Last resort for severe cases
